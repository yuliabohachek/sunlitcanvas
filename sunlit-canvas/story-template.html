48 Hours in Tokyo: Temples, Ramen & Neon Lights

Back to Stories
March 12, 2024 6 min read Tokyo, Japan

The moment the train doors opened at Shinjuku station, the city hit me with full force — a wall of neon, noise, and the quiet electricity of eight million people moving with purpose. I had 48 hours. Tokyo had centuries.

Morning: The silent temples

Before the city woke up, I made my way to Senso-ji temple in Asakusa. The stone path was still cool from the night, the incense smoke curling upward in the grey morning light. A monk swept the courtyard with the unhurried rhythm of someone who has always been here.

Senso-ji at dawn — the quietest hour in the loudest city

"Tokyo teaches you that stillness and chaos are not opposites — they are the same city at different hours."

Afternoon: Finding the light

By noon the streets had filled. I lost myself in the back alleys of Yanaka — one of the few neighbourhoods that survived the bombs and the earthquakes, where cats sleep on warm stone walls and shops sell things nobody needs but everyone wants.

Evening: Ramen and neon

There are 73 Michelin-starred restaurants in Tokyo — more than any other city on earth. I ate at none of them. Instead, I squeezed into a counter with eight seats in Shinjuku and ordered what the man next to me was having. The broth arrived dark and serious. It was the best thing I tasted all year.

The city never quite goes dark. Even at midnight, the convenience stores glow like small suns, the vending machines hum, and somewhere below the street a train moves silently through the dark.

The takeaway

48 hours is not enough for Tokyo. But then again, neither is a lifetime. The city keeps its best secrets for the people who return — and I already know I will.
